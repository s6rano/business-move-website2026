# How is a corporate move quote calculated?

**You receive two quotes for the same office move, and the amounts differ widely — sometimes twofold. It is hard to tell which one is right, and above all what it rests on. Understanding how a professional builds their price means you can read an offer, compare it and spot what is missing.**

Here is, item by item, how a corporate move quote is built in Belgium, and what to check before signing.

> A reliable quote is not given over the phone: it rests on a site visit and an item-by-item breakdown.

## It all starts with the site visit

A reliable quote starts from a visit on site. The mover comes to assess the real volume, access to both buildings (floors, lift, corridor width, room to park a lorry), the IT equipment to disconnect and reconnect, the archives to transport, and the schedule you want.

A price quoted from a simple description over the phone is worth little: it rests on assumptions, not on your reality. It is the visit that turns your situation into a figure — and commits the provider to that figure.

## How a volume becomes a price

On the Belgian market, two pricing logics dominate, often combined.

The first is time spent: an hourly rate multiplied by the number of movers. As a rough guide, you often see figures of €100 to €200 per hour for a team of two to four movers with a lorry, more with a furniture hoist. From the visit, the mover estimates the loading and unloading time, then the number of people needed.

The second is volume: a price per cubic metre, based on the volume estimated during the visit.

For offices, a third logic often comes on top: the cost per workstation. Expect around €100 to €300 per workstation depending on the equipment — clearly more if you have servers or specific hardware. It is a handy first approach, provided you do not forget access, IT and out-of-hours work.

## The priced items one by one

A corporate quote breaks down. Here is what the mover estimates behind each line:

- labour: the number of movers multiplied by the estimated hours — the main item;
- transport: number of trips, size and number of lorries, distance, fuel;
- packing: the supplies (boxes, protection, IT crates) and the time to pack;
- difficult handling: a furniture hoist, charged separately when access requires it;
- IT: disconnection, secure transport and reconnection, sometimes priced per workstation;
- insurance: based on the declared value of the goods transported;
- out-of-hours surcharges: evening, weekend or public holiday.

## What makes two quotes differ

Two offers can both be correct and still be far apart. The reason rarely lies in the "price of the lorry": it lies in the scope.

An "à la carte" quote charges only for transport and leaves packing, dismantling or IT to you. A full package includes them. On top of that come different assumptions (estimated volume, expected duration), a variable level of insurance, and services that are included or not (reinstating the old premises, for example). Comparing two amounts without looking at what they cover therefore makes no sense.

## How to read and compare a quote

A few reflexes avoid unpleasant surprises.

Insist on a detailed quote, broken down item by item. A mover who only presents a lump-sum figure, without being able to explain what it covers, is a bad sign.

Then make sure the quotes rest on the same basis: same volume, same date, same services. That is the only way to make them comparable.

Finally, ask the right questions before signing: has the site visit actually been done? What exactly does the insurance cover? Is the furniture hoist included? Is IT handled? Are there out-of-hours surcharges? Request at least three quotes after a visit.

Even before the quotes, you can frame your budget:

## In summary

A corporate move quote is not a figure pulled out of thin air: it is built from a site visit, an hourly rate or a volume-based price, and a set of items you can read one by one. Insist on the detail, compare on the same basis, and use the calculator for a first estimate before requesting three quotes.
