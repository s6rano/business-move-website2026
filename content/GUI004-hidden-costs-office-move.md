# The hidden costs of an office move: before, during and after the move day

**You have a detailed quote, and yet the final bill will be higher. That is normal: the transport price is only part of the true cost of an office move. Most of it happens around the edges — before, during and after the move day — in expenses you had not seen coming.**

A corporate move is never just about shifting furniture from one place to another, however well done. Here are the costs that stay in the shadows, and how to anticipate them.

> The quote is not the bill. The hidden costs of an office move sit mainly in what comes before and after the transport.

## Before the move day: the costs you do not anticipate

The bulk of the work begins long before the first box moves. Scoping the project, drawing up a brief, arranging the site visits and obtaining several quotes takes time — often your teams' time.

To this are added concrete expenses: sorting and decluttering, sometimes with paid disposal; IT preparation (backups, network planning); permits (parking, furniture hoist, sometimes a licence); internal communication; the switch-over schedule; negotiating or terminating the current lease; and fitting out the new site before the teams arrive. All of these are paid in time or in external services, before the move itself.

## During the move day: the extra costs that surface

On the day itself, it is the unexpected that pushes the bill up. A broken lift or a blocked parking space, and you need an unplanned furniture hoist. An operation that overruns, and it means overtime. A need for extra materials, damage to a piece of furniture or equipment — hence the importance of insurance.

These extra costs never appear in a "keenest-price" quote. They surface in practice, when it is too late to negotiate them.

## After the move day: the costs of getting back up

Once installed, the budget keeps running. Reinstating the old premises is often required by the lease, which demands a handover in "good condition", or even "perfect condition": painting, repairs, removing the fit-out. This work often comes to around €200 per m².

The new site has its cost too: technical reinstallation (IT network, telephony, connections, commissioning), new or adapted furniture, and the recurring charges (electricity, security, internet, cleaning) — often in the region of €80 to €100 per m². Finally, the settling-in period brings a drop in productivity over the first few weeks: an invisible but genuine cost to your results.

## The most underestimated cost: internal time

One item appears on no quote and yet weighs heavily: the hours your teams spend on the project. Scoping the need, comparing offers, coordinating providers, running the day itself, managing the restart — all of it takes people who already have a job.

The bigger the project, the more that time turns into an actual cost: delays, decisions made in a hurry, oversights that prove expensive later. It is often the heaviest item, and the least visible.

## How to avoid unpleasant surprises

Three reflexes limit the damage. Plan ahead: a schedule set early avoids rush and out-of-hours work. Draw up a full brief: it forces you to think through the before and the after, not just the transport. Request three comparable quotes, on the same basis.

The hardest part remains: spotting the blind spots when you have never run this kind of project. That is where an outside eye that organises and coordinates the move is most useful — it knows the costs that hide before and after the move day, and puts them on the table before they become surprises.

## In summary

The real cost of an office move goes well beyond the price of the lorry. It builds up before (preparation, permits, fit-out), during (unexpected events, overtime) and after (reinstatement, reinstallation, loss of productivity), not counting internal time. Anticipating them — alone or with a coordinator — keeps the final bill from doubling the quote.
